# Phase 00: Discovery

## Purpose

Understand the problem space before committing resources. Discovery answers three questions: Is there a real problem? Is the problem worth solving? Can we solve it better than alternatives?

## When to Enter

- A new product idea surfaces from leadership, customers, or the team.
- A market shift invalidates previous assumptions.
- A retrospective reveals an unmet user need.
- A competitor move creates urgency to explore a space.

## Entry Criteria

- A problem hypothesis exists, even if informal ("We believe X is a problem for Y").
- A sponsor or stakeholder has agreed to allocate discovery time.
- Access to target users or credible proxy data is available.

## Exit Criteria (Quality Gate)

Feasibility report approved by sponsor with an explicit go/no-go decision and documented rationale. The team can articulate the target user, core problem, and high-level solution direction.

## Key Skills

| Skill | Purpose | Effort |
|---|---|---|
| [market-analysis](market-analysis/SKILL.md) | Map the competitive landscape and size the opportunity | medium |
| [user-research](user-research/SKILL.md) | Build evidence-based understanding of target users | large |
| [feasibility-study](feasibility-study/SKILL.md) | Make a defensible go/no-go decision | medium |

## Typical Duration

- Startup / small team: 1-2 weeks
- Mid-size product org: 2-4 weeks
- Enterprise: 4-8 weeks

Duration scales with uncertainty. A well-understood adjacent feature needs less discovery than a new market entry.

## Skill Sequence

```
market-analysis ──┐
                  ├──> feasibility-study
user-research  ───┘
```

Market analysis and user research can run in parallel. Feasibility study consumes outputs from both.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Solution-first thinking.** Jumping to "let's build X" before validating the problem exists. Discovery is about the problem, not the solution.
- **Analysis paralysis.** Spending months in discovery to avoid commitment. Time-box discovery and make a decision with imperfect information.
- **Skipping discovery entirely.** "We already know what to build" is the most expensive assumption in product development.
- **Confirmation bias.** Designing research to validate a predetermined conclusion. Seek disconfirming evidence actively.
- **Stakeholder-only input.** Treating internal opinions as user research. Talk to actual users or rely on behavioral data.
